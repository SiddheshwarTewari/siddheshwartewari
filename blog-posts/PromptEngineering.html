The Art of Prompt Engineering

4 min read By Sid Tewari

My first interaction with a large language model was… underwhelming. I typed a command like I was talking to a stubborn search engine: "summary article climate change."

The result was a generic, bland paragraph that felt like it was copied from a middling high school essay. I was disappointed. Was this the revolutionary AI I’d heard so much about?

Then, I saw someone online generate a breathtaking piece of poetry, a functional code snippet, and a detailed business plan, all from the same tool. The difference wasn't the AI; it was the human. The difference was prompt engineering.

Phase 1: The Keyword Bully (And Why It Failed)

My initial approach was what I now call "The Keyword Bully." I’d keep shouting the same words, louder and with more urgency, in a single line:

  • Write a story.
  • Write a GOOD story.
  • Write a LONG, GOOD story about a dragon.

The results were slightly better, but still mediocre. The stories were cliché, the dragon was straight out of a cartoon, and the prose was flat. I was treating the AI like a vending machine: press button, get snack. But this wasn't a vending machine; it was a collaborative partner that needed context.

Lesson Learned: Specificity is not about adding more adjectives; it's about providing a framework for creativity.

Phase 2: Discovering the Power of Context and Persona

The breakthrough came when I stumbled upon the concept of role-playing. Instead of asking for a story, I started to set the stage. I learned how to write prompts like:


  "You are a renowned fantasy author known for your gritty, realistic worlds and morally complex characters. 
   Write the first chapter of a novel about a dragon who is the last guardian of a magical forest that is being destroyed by humans. 
   Focus on the dragon's internal conflict between its peaceful nature and its duty to protect its home."

The result was night and day. The AI’s output suddenly had voice, tone, and nuance. It understood the assignment because I had finally given it a proper brief. I wasn't just asking for what; I was defining the who, the how, and the why.

Lesson Learned: Assigning a persona to the AI is the single biggest lever for improving output quality. You are the director, and the AI is the world's most versatile actor.

Phase 3: The Technical Leap

Chain-of-Thought: I learned that for complex reasoning, you must ask the AI to think step-by-step.

  • Bad Prompt: Solve: A bakery sold 78 cookies in one day. If they sold 3 times as many chocolate chip as oatmeal raisin, and 10 more sugar cookies than oatmeal raisin, how many of each did they sell?
  • Good Prompt: Let's solve this problem step by step. Let O be the number of oatmeal raisin cookies. Then, the number of chocolate chip is 3O. The number of sugar cookies is O + 10. The total is O + 3O + (O + 10) = 78. Now, solve for O. [continue]...

Lesson Learned: Prompt engineering is software engineering for the mind. You are architecting a thought process.

Phase 4: The Ethical Dimension

With great power comes great responsibility. I began to see the dark side. I learned how easily prompts could be manipulated to bypass safety filters ("jailbreaking") or could subtly reinforce biases by how they were framed.

I made a rule for myself: Don't prompt for anything you wouldn't want to see on the front page of a newspaper. I shifted from trying to "break" the AI to trying to elicit its most helpful, harmless, and honest self.

  • Set boundaries: "Provide a balanced overview of [topic], citing arguments from multiple reputable sources."
  • Encourage uncertainty: "If you are not 95% confident of the answer, please state that you are unsure."
  • Cite sources: "When providing information, please include links or citations to reputable sources where possible."

Lesson Learned: The prompt engineer is a guardian of truth and ethics, not just a maximizer of output.

Conclusion: Its a Conversation, not a Command

I used to think prompt engineering was about finding the "magic words" to trick the AI into giving a good answer. I was wrong.

I’ve learned it’s the opposite. It's about clear, structured, and empathetic communication. It's about reducing ambiguity and building a shared understanding. It's a skill that makes me not just a better AI user, but a better thinker, writer, and collaborator.
